Domain 7 Key Takeaways

If you remember nothing else...

You cannot plan during disruption to continuity. Automated security testing facilitates test execution repeatedly, consistently, and rapidly at scale.

Summary

Overall, security professionals reviewing CSSLP Domain 7 must understand that the absence of controls, or inadequate implementation of safeguards during software integration, installation, and deployment stages, can adversely impact the operational security of software. Change controls (e.g., secure configurations) and access management concepts such as least privilege are critical to maintaining operational security.

Production application security challenges arise when organizational policies and procedures do not include considerations for production aspects of the SDLC. Policies and procedures help ensure production risks are managed consistently and addressed appropriately. Effective and timely response to system security incidents requires planning and preparation. You cannot plan during disruption to continuity.

Post-deployment security checks are important for establishing system confidence and to understand operational security posture. Automated security testing facilitates test execution repeatedly, consistently, and rapidly at scale. Some enterprises are vast and continuous monitoring can present challenges though modern automated tools and technologies can facilitate implementation.

Performing operational risk analysis 

  • Operational risks are synonymous with production risks for software systems.
  • Providing appropriate training to personnel is an opportunity for risk treatment by increasing awareness within the organization.
  • Staging and conducting software assurance—the level of confidence that software is free from vulnerabilities either intentionally designed in or accidentally inserted at any time during its life cycle, and that the software functions as intended1—are important to ensure that software will function as desired before deploying to production.
  • ISO 31004 defines guidelines for implementing risk management and highlights the importance of integrating risk management with business processes.
  • ISO 31022 provides guidance to enable organizations to manage the complexities of legal compliance.

Securing configuration and version control

  • Security Configuration Management (SecCM) is the management and control of configurations for an information system to enable security and facilitate risk management.
  • Configuration Management (CM) encompasses establishing and maintaining the integrity of software products and systems by controlling processes for adding, modifying, and monitoring configurations.
  • CM Plans typically include:
    • Change Control Board (CCB). This is comprised of parties responsible for controlling and approving changes throughout the lifecycle software systems.
    • Configuration Item Identification. This covers selection and the naming method for CI.
    • Configuration Change Control. This is the process for managing baseline configuration changes.
    • Configuration Monitoring. This is the process for assessing baseline compliance that includes reporting.
  • A baseline configuration is a set of reviewed and accepted configuration items for a system that becomes the basis for builds, releases, and production conformance.
  • Configuration change control should apply to all change scenarios—whether an emergency or unscheduled business need or routine updates.
  • SecCM policies should establish expectations for the organization using specific, measurable, and testable objectives.
  • The CM Plan is operational and describes how SecCM policy (i.e., strategy) should be implemented. 

Storing and managing security data

  • Secure deployment of software also needs to address protection of secrets (e.g., passwords and tokens) required for applications to operate in production environments.
  • Depending on the maturity of organizational processes, you may need to:
    • Introduce protection measures to limit access to production secrets.
    • Inject secrets dynamically during deployment process from hardened storages and auditing all human access to them.
    • Improve the life cycle of application secrets by regularly generating them and by ensuring proper use.
  • Deploying encryption and other security solutions across the enterprise requires managing secrets. Vaults are services that securely manage secrets for applications.

Ensuring secure installation 

  • Secure installation can include the following activities:
    • Appropriate security hardening across all parts of the application stack
    • Disabling/removing unnecessary features
    • Disabling/changing default accounts and their passwords
    • Enabling or configuring latest security features for upgraded systems
    • Choosing appropriate security settings in application server, application frameworks, libraries, and databases
    • Adhering to the principle of least privilege 
    • Appropriate permissions and access control lists (ACLs)
    • Tamper-resistant distribution media
  • Security policy should address the following software installation and deployment issues:
    • Password complexity, aging, and expiration
    • Granting privileges to roles and not users while adhering to the least-privilege principles
    • Secure generation and protection of encryption keys
    • Secure configuration settings for the software 

Obtaining security approval to operate

  • The risk management strategy typically describes the following:
    • Risk assessment (e.g., tools, techniques, procedures, and methodologies)
    • Risks Evaluation (e.g., severity or criticality)
    • Risk aggregation and categorization from information systems and other sources
    • Risk response approaches
    • Risk tolerance
    • Risk monitoring
  • When security is a concern, planning the deployment of an IT product or service within an existing system may involve a formal process to obtain permission, an authorization to operate (ATO) for a specified period.
  • Risk acceptance is the responsibility of an authorizing or authorizing official (AO) while considering various factors.
  • Risk acceptance is the explicit acceptance of risk and is the responsibility of an authorizing or accepting official who holds a senior management position with the authority and technical knowledge to make such a decision.  

Performing information security continuous monitoring 

  • Continuous monitoring provides for better risk management decision making, and it helps with anomaly detection, incident response, compliance with regulatory requirements, and forensics analysis.
  • Security monitoring helps achieve various objectives, including:
    • Better understanding of the current security posture resulting in better security decision making
    • Early detection of attacks, better response to attacks, and reduction of impact from attacks
    • Detection of violations of internal security policies or standards
    • Compliance with regulatory requirements such as HIPAA, SOX, and GDPR
    • Digital forensics analysis
    • Reauthorization (as highlighted when discussing ATOs earlier)
  • When possible, organizations should look for automated solutions to lower costs, enhance efficiency, and improve the reliability of monitoring security-related information.

Executing the incident response plan

  • A security incident is an occurrence that actually or potentially jeopardizes the confidentiality, integrity, or availability of an information system or the information the system processes, stores, or transmits or that constitutes a violation or imminent threat of violation of security policies, security procedures, or acceptable use policies.
  • Incident response life cycle has four phases: Preparation, Detection & Analysis, Containment Eradication & Recovery, and Post-Incident Activities.
  • Root cause analysis (RCA) is a method of problem solving that looks past the symptoms to identify the underlying events and problems.
  • Computer Forensics, the gathering of computer evidence, includes collection, examination, analysis, and reporting. 

Performing patch management

  • Patch management is the process for identifying, acquiring, installing, and verifying patches for products and systems.
  • Patches correct security and functionality and security problems in software and firmware.
  • Patch management challenges include:
    • Multiple mechanisms for applying patches (e.g., manual update, automatic update, third-party patch-management solutions)
    • Dependency on software inventory, without which, correct patches cannot be identified
    • Patch side-effects (e.g., patch may alter existing security configuration settings)
    • Patch implementation verification (e.g., reboot required)
    • Conflicts between application whitelisting technologies and patch management technologies 

Performing vulnerability management

  • Vulnerability management is a key component in planning for and determining the appropriate implementation of controls and the management of risk.
  • Vulnerability management involves:
    • Defining a vulnerability analysis and resolution strategy
    • Developing a plan for vulnerability management
    • Implementing the vulnerability analysis and resolution capability
    • Assessing and improving the capability

Incorporating runtime protection

  • Controls are needed to protect applications at runtime such as web application firewalls (WAFs), address space layout randomization (ASLR), and Data Execution Prevention (DEP).
  • A WAF protects infrastructure by analyzing and filtering network traffic between the application and the internet resource requestor; WAFs serve as a perimeter safeguard that blocks malicious traffic before it reaches the application.
  • The address space layout randomization (ASLR) feature makes it difficult to create buffer-overflow attacks by randomizing the memory address for executables at system boot.
  • DEP is a set of hardware and software technologies that perform additional checks on memory to prevent malicious code from running on a system.
  • Runtime Application Self-Protection (RASP) describes incorporating application security functionality within the software at runtime to reduce susceptibility to malicious events during execution. 

Supporting continuity of operations

  • Continuity planning is often subject to internal and external compliance requirements and may involve an initial business impact analysis (BIA) to identify those mission-critical services.
  • Minimizing the effects of outages and disruptions on business operations is the key driver for continuity planning. Organizations may have separate business continuity (BC) and disaster recovery (DR) plans or may have a joint BC/DR plan.
  • The Business Continuity Plan (BCP) focuses on sustaining an organization’s mission/business processes during and after a disruption.
  • A Disaster Recovery Plan (DRP) applies to major, usually physical disruptions to service that deny access to the primary facility infrastructure for an extended period. A DRP is an information system-focused plan designed to restore operability of the target system, application, or computer facility infrastructure at an alternate site after an emergency.
  • Maximum Tolerable Downtime (MTD). MTD represents the total amount of time the system owner/authorizing official is willing to accept for a mission/business process outage.
  • Recovery Time Objective (RTO). RTO defines the maximum amount of time that a system resource can remain unavailable before there is an unacceptable impact on other system resources, supported mission/business processes, and the MTD.
  • Recovery Point Objective (RPO). RPO represents the point in time, prior to a disruption or system outage, to which mission/business process data can be recovered (given the most recent backup copy of the data) after an outage.
  • Common system backups methods include:
    • A full backup captures all files on the disk or within the folder selected for backup.
    • An incremental backup captures files created or changed since the last backup, regardless of backup type.
    • A differential backup stores files created or modified since the last full backup.
