Misuse and Abuse Cases (3.6)

Objectives

  • Describe misuse and abuse cases and their relevance to known attack patterns.

Misuse and Abuse Cases

Use cases are undoubtedly helpful when it comes to eliciting software functional requirements and reaching requirements maturation but for security practitioners this is only half the equation. To engineer trustworthy, secure systems, practitioners must also advocate for abuse and misuse cases. Each phase of the secure software life cycle has its own relevant security activities. Various security-oriented methodologies (e.g., Microsoft SDL) regard misuse and as an effective tool for modeling security requirements.

To contrast use cases, misuse and abuse cases incapsulate thinking with the perspective of malicious subjects aiming to inflict damage rather than end-user activities. When considering the security of an application, abuse cases can be indispensable. They can aid development teams by identifying specific attacks against the application. With these attacks identified:

  • The business risk from each corresponding attack can be evaluated.
  • Security requirements can be developed.
  • Safeguards and countermeasures can be determined.
  • Impact on the project timelines, schedules and resources can be better estimated.

Notion of an Abuse Case

OWASP describes the notion of abuse cases as “a way to use a feature that was not expected by the implementer, allowing an attacker to influence the feature or outcome of use of the feature based on the attacker action (or input).”25

The software company Synopsys, Inc., enumerates various ways that security requirements can be driven by abuse cases: “Misuse and abuse cases describe how users can misuse or exploit weak controls in software features to attack an application. A direct attack against business functionalities, which may bring in revenue or provide a positive user experience, can have a tangible business impact. Abuse cases can be an effective way to drive security requirements to properly protect these critical business use cases.”26

An example of an abuse case could be adding large quantities of an item to a shopping cart to reduce the buying opportunity for other users. Another possibility would be using automated means (e.g., botnets) to create shopping carts and make periodic item reservations in large quantities.

Developing can also benefit the security testing phase of the life cycle, where test cases can be created to assure protection against identified abuse scenarios.

Process to Develop Misuse and Abuse Cases

The process for developing meaningful or useful misuse cases is not trivial. It requires the ability to think like attackers and to be experienced with attack patterns, among other relevant skills.

The process involves development of use cases as the first step, followed by creation of data-flow diagrams (DFDs) for implementation of the use cases. With the understanding of the data flows, potential threats are subsequently identified, and misuse cases based on the identified threats are enumerated. At this stage, the knowledge of the relevant attack patterns must be used to derive the “abusive interaction” with the system to create the misuse cases.

Dictionary of Known Attack Patterns

A dictionary of known patterns of attack commonly utilized by bad actors can aid in understanding how common attacks work (i.e., understanding the anatomy of an attack).

Common Attack Pattern Enumeration and Classification (CAPEC) is a good example of a publicly available catalog of common attack patterns, where patterns are presented by the mechanism or the domain of attacks. CAPEC was developed by the U.S. Department of Homeland Security.

Some well-known attack patterns include:

  • HTTP Response Splitting (CAPEC-34)
  • Session Fixation (CAPEC-61)
  • Cross Site Request Forgery (CAPEC-62)
  • SQL Injection (CAPEC-66)
  • Cross-Site Scripting (CAPEC-63)
  • Buffer Overflow (CAPEC-100)
  • Clickjacking (CAPEC-103)
  • Relative Path Traversal (CAPEC-139)
  • XML Attribute Blowup (CAPEC-229)

MITRE Adversarial Tactics, Techniques, and Common Knowledge (ATT&CK)

ATT&CK is a project started in 2013 by The MITRE Corporation to document common tactics, techniques, and procedures used by advanced persistent threats.

ATT&CK is largely a knowledge base of adversarial techniques—a breakdown and classification of offensively oriented actions that can be used against platforms such as Windows. Unlike prior work in this area, the focus isn’t on the tools and malware that adversaries use but on how they interact with systems during an operation.27

ATT&CK Techniques and Tactics

MITRE describes techniques and tactics as follows:

  • Techniques. These are how an adversary may achieve its objective by performing an action. They may also represent what an adversary gains by performing an action. An example of a technique to gain initial foothold within a network would be “Drive-by Compromise.” Another example would be taking advantage of a weakness (e.g., a bug, a glitch, or a design vulnerability) in an internet-facing system/software.
  • Tactics. These are the why of an ATT&CK technique. They cover things that adversaries do during an operation: discover information, move laterally, execute files, and exfiltrate data.

The relationship between tactics and techniques can be visualized in the ATT&CK matrices. Currently, matrices for common platforms (e.g., Windows, macOS, Linux, AWS, Azure) are available.
