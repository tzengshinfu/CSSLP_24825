Domain 1 Key Takeaways

If you remember nothing else...

Security practitioners must ensure that the implementation of secure software system design results in a consistent, repeatable process that can be measured and monitored across the system life cycle.

The better the secure design implementation accomplishes adequate security the less dependent the production systems and software will be on the addition of security control safeguards (i.e., Do security sooner rather than later).

Summary

Overall, security practitioners reviewing CSSLP Domain 1 must develop a comprehensive understanding regarding the core security objectives of confidentiality, integrity, and availability (CIA), as well as privacy considerations. The understanding of these organizational needs drives the formulation of requirements for security in software. Secure software concepts inform architectural decisions independent of platform specifics or programming language. These principles and abstraction establish the foundation for building secure software. Regardless of framework, standard, or methodology selection, security practitioners must ensure that the implementation of secure system and software design results in a consistent, repeatable process that can be measured and monitored across the life cycle. At the heart of every security endeavor are considerations for confidentiality, integrity, and availability—software security is no exception.

Applications must be built with business and security goals from the beginning of the system engineering life cycle. Governance, Risk, and compliance (GRC) integrates business, risk, and compliance considerations into a manageable process. Production applications have added complexities such as jurisdiction and resource allocations within systems that are best considered as early as possible within the development life cycle. The better the secure design implementation accomplishes adequate security the less dependent the production systems and software will be on the addition of security control safeguards.

Understanding core concepts

  • Confidentiality involves preventing unauthorized disclosure.
    • Encryption commonly supports confidentiality.
  • Integrity involves preventing unauthorized changes.
    • Hashing and code signing commonly support integrity.
  • Availability involves ensuring reliable and timely access.
    • Redundancy, failover, fault tolerance, RAID, and Replication support availability.
  • Access controls support confidentiality and integrity. Access control is based on identification, authentication, authorization, and accountability—IAAA.
    • Identification: I am Jiang.
    • Authentication: Prove I am Jiang.
    • Authorization: Jiang is allowed to do XYZ.
    • Accountability: Jiang’s actions are logged and non-repudiation.
  • Governance, Risk, and Compliance (GRC) is a discipline method to correlate business objectives and IT infrastructure while controlling risks by addressing regulations.
  • Governance establishes policies and procedures for achieving business goals.
  • Risk management identifies and then reduces risk to an acceptable level.
  • Compliance is ensuring that the organization identifies and satisfies any applicable laws and regulations.
  • Software regulatory considerations make GRC a complex, non-trivial endeavor.
    • Jurisdiction is the power of the court to decide legal issues or execute legal orders and is tied to geographic area and the nationality of the software, data, users, and owners 

Understanding security design principles

  • Adequate security represents intentional trade-offs to reach a state of security that supports the business function of the system or software.
  • Characteristics of a secure system include:
    • It enables system capability despite adverse operating conditions.
    • It constrains functions to desired behaviors based on required system capabilities.
    • It enforces constraints via predefined rulesets to ensure only authorized interactions and operations are allowed.
  • Least privilege is the concept of providing no more authorizations than necessary to perform the required functions.
  • Zero Trust is a collection of concepts and ideas designed to minimize uncertainty in enforcing accurate, least privilege per-request access decisions in information systems and services in the face of a network viewed as compromised
  • Tenants of Zero Trust:
    • Data sources and computing services are resources
    • All Communication is secured
    • Access to resources is granted individually on a per-session basis
    • Access to resources is determined by dynamic policy
    • Continuously monitor and measure the integrity and security posture of assets
    • Authentication and authorization are dynamic and strictly enforced
    • Collects adequate data to improve and maintain security posture
  • Separation of duties is the concept that no subject should be given enough privileges to misuse the system on their own.
  • Defense-in-depth involves applying multiple layers of protection where a subsequent layer will provide protection if a different layer is breached.
  • Economy of mechanism suggests security solutions should be as simple as possible.
  • The concept of open design dictates that the security of a system should not be dependent on secrecy of its design, its implementation, or its components.
  • Modular Open Systems Approach (MOSA) is a technical and business strategy for designing adaptable systems including software.
  • Open-Source Software (OSS) libraries and other products are not inherently secure just because the source code is freely available.
