Domain 8 Key Takeaways

If you remember nothing else...

Outsourcing and offshoring arrangements for software development may result in efficiency gains at the cost of control and visibility into the software development practices and processes.

Proactive security is better. Establish responsibilities and assurances upfront contractually rather than resorting to periodic organizationally defined audits and scans as a bolt on process post software supplier adoption.

Summary

Overall, security professionals reviewing CSSLP Domain 8 should understand outsourcing and offshoring arrangements for software development, may result in efficiency gains at the cost of control and visibility into the software development practices and processes. When applications are constructed using many third-party software components (proprietary or open source), vulnerabilities in those components may be inherited by the applications. Software supply chain risks must be understood and managed.

A source code repository represents valuable intellectual property—data at rest. However, the development environment must also be secure, and software must be distributed and transferred securely which represents protection for valuable data in transit. Additionally, when acquiring software, organizations must address ownership of intellectual properties, code escrow, liabilities and warranties, and service-level agreements.

Suppliers must be categorized and evaluated based on their criticality to the organization's mission. Building a level of confidence in the security of acquired software may require an audit of the third party’s secure SDLC policy and practices. Vulnerability and incident notification and reporting, maintenance and support structure, licensing, and vendors' security records are considerations during acquisitions.

Mitigating risk early in the life cycle is always a security prerogative. It is better to establish responsibilities and assurances upfront contractually which fosters supply chain trust rather than resorting to periodic organizationally defined audits and scans as a bolt on process post software supplier adoption.

Implementing software supply chain risk management

  • Risks can be introduced at any point in the supply chain and may be inherited by each subsequent acquisition.
  • Organizational hardware, software, and systems are susceptible to significant risk if proper mitigating actions are not taken by security practitioners.
  • Cybersecurity Supply-Chain Risk Management (C-SCRM) addresses cybersecurity risks that occur through the various stages of the supply chain arising from the use of software and services.
  • Third-party component (TPC) management includes:
    • Maintain a list of TPCs.
    • Assess security risks from TPCs.
    • Mitigate or accept risks arising from vulnerable TPCs.
    • Monitor changes to ensure that the risk profile remains acceptable over time.
  • Software Bill of Materials (SBOM) is a formal record containing the details and supply chain relationships of various components used in building software.
  • SBOM Attributes:
    • Supplier name 
    • Supplier’s original software name
    • Supplier’s version identifier
    • Unique identifiers
    • Dependencies (i.e., component X is included in software Y)
    • SBOM author’s name and information
    • Time and date of SBOM creation 

Analyzing security of third-party software

  • The Software Component Verification Standard (SCVS) establishes a framework for identifying activities, controls, and best practices, which can help in identifying and reducing risk in a software supply chain.
  • One risk is intentional supply chain disruption such as hostile vendor acquisition by foreign interests.
  • Software supply chain attacks are particularly bothersome and insidious because they violate the basic and assumed trust between software provider and consumer.
  • There are many acceptable means of validation that include periodic reassessment such as self-attestation, site visits, third-party assessments, and certifications order from least to most rigor.
  • The level of supplier rigor should align with the criticality of the software or service being acquired with respect to the inheriting organization. 

Verifying pedigree and provenance

  • A component’s pedigree (sometimes referred to as ‘provenance’) refers to the traceability of all changes (i.e., commits), releases, modifications, packaging, and distribution across the entire supply chain,
  • Provenance == physical supply chain, “chain of custody”

Ensuring and verifying supplier security requirements in the acquisition process

  • Dealing with software suppliers...
    • Purchase only trustworthy software from trustworthy suppliers by:
      • Establishing sufficient basis for trusting suppliers and their products
      • Discovering and trace pedigree and provenance of software products (including components from supplier contractors or third parties)
      • Requiring assurance cases from software suppliers
    • Prior to acquisition:
      • Analyses should be taken to detect indicators of counterfeiting, piracy, and license violations.
      • There should be an examination to detect malicious logic, backdoors, vulnerabilities
    • Auditing software suppliers:
      • Establishing a security baseline through annual audits
      • Spelling out your objectives
      • Choosing auditors with real security experience
  • Organizational policies and controls should reflect vendor importance when performing third-party security due diligence and ensuring compliance with the security policy.  Think risk impact vs component criticality.
  • Software licensing is a risk factor. Some open-source licensing such as copyleft may be deemed riskier than permissive.
    • Public domain software can be used and modified by anyone with no restrictions; hence, it is the most permissive type of software license.
    • Permissive licenses have minimal requirements for modification or redistribution.
    • Copyleft licenses, also known as restrictive licenses, mean that any software created or modified under that license must be distributed under the same license terms.
  • A right-to-audit clause in a contract is a key security control for supply chain but does not mean the organization must perform an audit; it merely reserves the right to perform an audit if needed. 

Supporting contractual requirements

  • When the development of software is outsourced, intellectual property (IP) rights and ownership must be addressed using contract language.
  • There are usually three parties involved in a software escrow, or source code escrow including:
    • The acquirer or licensee that is purchasing the software
    • The publisher or licensor that develops the software
    • A neutral third party that holds the copies of source code as insurance
  • Contract language establishes expectations and liabilities which mitigate undue risk.
  • One liability includes warranty terms and conditions such as:
    • The exact nature of the items that that the developer or licensor warrants
    • Remedies available in case of the breach of the warranty
    • The scope and duration of the warranty
  • Service-Level Agreements (SLA) as a commitment between a service provider and one or more customers and addresses specific aspects of the service, such as responsibilities, details on the type of service, expected performance level, and requirements for reporting, resolution, and termination.
  • Within the SLA, a service-level objective (SLO) may be used to specify metrics that reflect a promise made to a customer (e.g., uptime, response time).
  • The service-level requirement (SLR) document contains the requirements for a service from the client viewpoint and describes detailed service targets, mutual responsibilities, and other requirements specific to a certain group of customers.
