Domain 8 Review

Software supply chains have the potential to include many linkages, with nuisances such as contract language and licensing terms at each integration point. GOTS, COTS, and OSS all have the potential to have supply chain vulnerabilities based on library and service dependencies. Market conditions have created a heavy reliance on third-party code, including outsourced development activities. The risk of exploitable vulnerabilities and embedded malicious code in software must be mitigated. Gaining access to assurance artifacts can be a non-trivial effort.

OWASP’s SCVS and SBOM can aid in identifying and reducing risk in a software supply chain. Safeguarding internal code repositories, including the development environment, is essential to reducing risk. Public repository security must also be vetted with a focus on genesis, contributor base, and change process. Code signing provides assurance that the software is authentic and has not been tampered with during the distribution and maintenance phases, and the verifier can validate these properties at runtime.

Suppliers must be categorized and evaluated based on their criticality to the organization's mission. Building a level of confidence in the security of acquired software may require an audit of the third party’s secure SDLC policy and practices. Further, intellectual property, code escrow, liability and warranty, and service-level agreements must be addressed when acquiring software. Vulnerability and incident notification and reporting, maintenance and support structure, licensing, and vendors' security track record are considerations during acquisitions. 

Mitigating risk early in the life cycle is always a security prerogative. It is better to establish responsibilities and assurances upfront contractually, which fosters supply chain trust rather than resorting to periodic organizationally defined audits and scans as a bolt-on process after adopting a software supplier.
